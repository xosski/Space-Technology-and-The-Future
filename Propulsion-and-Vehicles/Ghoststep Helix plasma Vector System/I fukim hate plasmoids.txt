In principle, yes—magnetic and electric fields can shape plasma, and plasma physicists already use carefully designed field geometries to confine, guide, and accelerate plasma. The catch is that plasma doesn't really behave like a liquid that keeps whatever shape you "mold" it into. It tends to expand, kink, reconnect, and become unstable.

Looking at your sketch:

The outer blue wave could be thought of as a lower-energy confinement layer or sheath.
The inner yellow sawtooth could represent regions of higher magnetic pressure, current density, or energy concentration.
The idea would be that the outer layer provides gross confinement while the inner structure deliberately introduces directional shaping forces.

Conceptually, you're describing something similar to a combination of:

Magnetic nozzles
Field-reversed configurations (FRCs)
Spheromaks
Rotating magnetic fields
Helical confinement systems

The interesting part is the sawtooth profile.

A sawtooth-like field distribution could theoretically create:

Directional pressure gradients
Strong compression on one side.
Expansion on another.
Net steering effect.
Localized acceleration zones
Plasma encountering repeated field "ramps" could gain directional momentum.
Plasmoid formation
Under the right conditions, the system might pinch off magnetized plasma packets.
These would be short-lived, not permanent.
Beam shaping
You might influence:
divergence
spread angle
rotation
focusing

What it would not do is create a perfectly rigid plasma orb.

The plasma remains:

hot
conductive
unstable
self-interacting

So the challenge becomes maintaining the magnetic topology that keeps the structure coherent.

For propulsion, a speculative "Lazarus Plasmoid Lance" style concept might work like:

Contain
→ Compress
→ Twist
→ Form plasmoid
→ Accelerate
→ Release

where:

Outer field = confinement
Inner sawtooth/helical field = energy injection and shaping
Exit nozzle = directional release

The result would be closer to a magnetized plasma projectile than a conventional rocket exhaust plume.

For ship maneuvering, the idea becomes even more interesting.

Instead of:

continuous thrust

you could imagine:

energy storage
→ rapid plasmoid release
→ impulse maneuver

Almost like a spacecraft version of a reaction-control thruster, but storing energy in a magnetically confined plasma reservoir and releasing it in bursts.

The biggest obstacle isn't the shaping concept itself—it's keeping the plasma stable long enough for the shaping to matter. Plasma has a nasty habit of turning carefully designed field structures into turbulence if the confinement isn't exceptionally good.

So the short answer is:

Yes, magnetic topology could theoretically be used to shape, steer, focus, and even eject coherent plasma structures. The shaping comes from the electromagnetic field geometry, not from the plasma mechanically holding its own shape. Your sawtooth concept is more plausible as a field-pressure modulation system than as a literal plasma "mold."
