POC A — Superconductor Hover Rail + Guided Transport
A0. What this POC proves

Hover/lock (flux pinning) over a magnetic guideway

Guided travel along a rail path

Propulsion via a coil “linear pull” track (simple linear motor)

This is the correct physics core for “train-through-a-ship.”

A1. Bill of Materials (POC scale: ~0.5–1.0 m track)
Levitation

YBCO superconductor puck (HTS disk)

Liquid nitrogen (LN₂) + small Dewar / LN₂-rated container

Thermal gloves + face shield (LN₂ safety)

Track / Guideway

Neodymium magnets (lots of identical ones)

Track base: wood/acrylic + a printed/laser-cut “magnet slot” spine

Sidewalls (optional but recommended): thin acrylic strips or printed guard rails

Propulsion (linear pull track)

10–20 identical coils (enameled copper wire wound on printed bobbins)

10–20 MOSFET driver channels (modules or a driver board)

Flyback diodes per coil (if not built into modules)

Controller: Arduino or ESP32

Power supply: typically 12–24V (depends on coil resistance)

Sensors (optional but helps a lot)

Hall sensors or IR break-beams to detect “car position” per segment

A2. Track geometry (stable, beginner-friendly)

For stable levitation you want the puck to “want” the center line.

Good POC geometry: a “magnetic trough”

Two magnet rows on the sides with opposing polarity, producing a restoring field toward center.

A third center row (optional) increases lift/locking.

If you want, I can sketch an exact polarity map (N/S layout) for your magnet size—just tell me magnet dimensions.

A3. The “car”

Simplest reliable build:

A small platform (light plastic/foam) with the YBCO puck mounted on it

Keep the puck close to the field (don’t bury it behind thick material)

Add a small steel/magnet “grab point” underneath if you’re using pull-coils for motion (details below)

A4. Propulsion options (choose one)
Option A (Recommended POC): “Segmented Pull-Coils”

You place coils along the track and energize them in sequence.

How it works

Your levitating platform carries a small ferromagnetic slug (or small magnet) below it.

Coil segment #1 turns on → pulls the slug forward a few cm → turns off.

Segment #2 turns on → pulls again → repeat.

This is easy, scalable, and good enough to prove “ship rail shuttle” dynamics.

Option B (Later upgrade): True linear synchronous motor

Smoother, faster, harder. Do this after A passes.

A5. Wiring (per coil segment)

Each coil segment:

Coil + supply

MOSFET low-side switching

Flyback diode across the coil

Optional current sense (nice-to-have)

Control pattern

Only energize one coil at a time (or two with overlap) to reduce heat and power spikes.

A6. Control logic (code skeleton)

Two modes:

Mode 1: Open-loop stepping (simplest)

Fixed pulse duration per coil (e.g., 50–150 ms)

Adjustable delay between pulses

Mode 2: Closed-loop stepping (best)

Hall sensor detects the slug arriving at segment N

Trigger segment N+1

Pseudo-flow (closed-loop)

Read sensors

If sensor N triggers → turn ON coil N+1 briefly

Turn OFF coil N after handoff

Repeat

If you tell me Arduino vs ESP32 and how many segments you want, I’ll give you a complete runnable sketch with pin map.

A7. Test plan (what “POC A passed” means)

Record these:

Hover time: > 60 seconds stable

Track adherence: stays within guideway without contact

Distance traveled: > 0.5 m under coil propulsion

Repeatability: 5 successful runs end-to-end

A8. Safety (non-negotiable)

LN₂ = cold burns + oxygen displacement risk → ventilate

Magnets pinch hard and can shatter → eye protection

Coils get hot → duty cycle limits

POC B — Plasma Thermal Addon (Layered onto A)
B0. What this addon proves

Not “plasma magically makes cryogenics.” Instead, something testable:

A controllable plasma discharge changes heat transfer from a target surface, and you can measure it.

This gives you a real datapoint for the “plasma modulation / thermal management” theme and plugs into your white-paper narrative as an experimental module.

B1. Safety-first constraint (important)

I’m not going to walk you through building a DIY high-voltage power supply from scratch.

Use a commercial / educational-rated plasma source, such as:

a DBD plasma demonstrator/kit sold for education/research, or

a commercial cold-plasma air treatment module with known ratings and enclosure

Goal: keep this as an instrument, not a hazard.

B2. Addon architecture

You add a “thermal target” near the rail environment:

Thermal target

Small aluminum plate (or copper) with a resistive heater pad

Temperature sensor: thermistor / RTD / thermocouple

Controlled heater power (so you can generate repeatable heat)

Plasma module positioning

Plasma discharge directed across/near the plate

You measure: temperature vs time at fixed heater power

B3. Measurement procedure (clean A/B test)

Run heater at fixed power until temperature stabilizes (baseline)

Turn plasma ON (same heater power)

Log temperature drop (or change in equilibrium temp)

Turn plasma OFF

Repeat 3–5 times

What you extract

ΔT at steady state (cooling effectiveness)

Time constant change (how quickly it cools)

Power cost (plasma watts vs thermal benefit)

B4. How it “adds onto A”

Once B works on the bench, you can mount the thermal target near:

coil segments (coil heating management),

enclosed rail tunnel airflow zone,

or a “superconductor chamber mockup” (NOT LN₂ itself—keep it separate).

This gives you a believable pathway:

A: superconducting hover transport is real and demonstrated

B: plasma modulation affects thermal behavior in a controllable way

Later: integrate thermal management intelligently (not magically)
