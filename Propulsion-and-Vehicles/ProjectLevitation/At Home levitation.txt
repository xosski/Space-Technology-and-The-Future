PoC: Spinning-Magnet Eddy-Current “Lift” Measured on a Scale (100 g target)
What you’re proving

A changing magnetic field induces eddy currents in a conductor (aluminum). Those currents create an opposing magnetic field → repulsive force. If your rotor generates an upward force on itself, it generates an equal downward force on the plate.

So you measure the effect by watching the scale reading increase.

Target: +100 g on the scale (≈ 0.98 N).

Parts you can source easily

Mechanical

1× DC motor (12–24 V “775 motor” style or similar) + simple speed controller (PWM).
The exact model isn’t sacred; you just want decent RPM and torque.

1× rotor disk (plywood, acrylic, or 3D printed), ~100–150 mm diameter

1× motor mount (wood blocks / L-brackets / scrap metal)

1× clear guard (polycarbonate/acrylic bowl or sheet) to cover the spinning rotor

Magnet + target

8–16× neodymium magnets (rectangular or arc segments; identical is best)

1× aluminum plate (thicker is better):

3–6 mm thick if possible

~200×200 mm or larger

Measuring

1× digital kitchen scale (1 g resolution is fine)

Feeler gauges / stack of cards / spacers (to set a consistent gap)

Fasteners + safety

Epoxy (high strength), or mechanical magnet retainers (highly recommended)

Eye protection, gloves

Threadlocker, zip ties, tape

Layout (top-down + side-view)
Top view (rotor disk)

Make a disk with magnets arranged around the perimeter like a ring:

Place magnets equally spaced around the edge.

Alternate polarity around the ring (N-S-N-S…) if you can identify poles.

This alternating pattern increases field “change” as it spins over the plate → more eddy currents.

Critical: The rotor never touches the plate. You want a controllable air gap.

Build steps (reproducible, not finicky)
1) Build the rotor safely (most important part)

Put magnets on the underside of the disk near the edge.

Do not rely on glue alone if you can avoid it:

Add a thin “retaining ring” (plastic/wood) over the magnets,

Or tape + epoxy + mechanical lip.

Balance matters. Keep spacing consistent.

Safety note: A thrown magnet at high RPM is nasty. Guard it like it’s trying to escape (it is).

2) Mount motor + set adjustable gap

Motor mounted rigidly above where the plate will sit.

Add an adjustable height mechanism:

stacked spacers, shims, or a simple screw-adjust platform.

Start with a large gap (like 10 mm) and move closer later.

3) Measurement setup (how to “see” 100 g clearly)

Put aluminum plate on the scale.

Tare the scale with the plate on it.

Position rotor centered above the plate (no contact).

Place a guard over rotor.

4) Test protocol (repeatable)

Baseline: rotor off → scale ~0 g (tared).

Turn rotor on at low speed → watch scale reading.

Increase speed gradually:

You should see the scale reading rise (e.g., +5 g → +20 g → +60 g…).

For each speed, record:

gap distance

scale reading increase

approximate RPM (optional; phone tach apps exist but not required)

To reach 100 g, you generally need:

small-ish gap (a few mm)

decent RPM

enough magnet strength and perimeter coverage

a thick-ish aluminum plate

Tuning knobs (what to change when you’re stuck)

If you’re only getting a few grams:

Increase effect

More magnets (increase ring density)

Stronger magnets

Smaller gap

Higher RPM

Larger rotor diameter (more edge speed)

Improve conductor coupling

Thicker aluminum plate (up to a point)

Keep the plate directly under the magnet path (big enough area)

Reduce losses

Keep rotor balanced to allow higher RPM safely

Improve mounting rigidity (wobble kills close gaps)

What counts as “Demonstrating 100 g”

If the scale reads +100 g (while tared with the plate), that means your system is applying ~0.98 N downward onto the plate, which corresponds to ~0.98 N upward on the rotor assembly (Newton’s 3rd).

That is a legitimate 100 g-force demonstration.

Safety checklist (don’t skip)

Guard over rotor (polycarbonate is best)

Eye protection

No loose clothing/hair

Start at large gap + low RPM

Keep hands away while spinning

Inspect magnets every run
