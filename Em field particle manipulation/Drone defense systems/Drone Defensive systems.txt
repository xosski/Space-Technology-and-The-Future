PoC: Dual-Channel EM Gating Field for Defensive EMI Containment and Drone-Adjacent Resilience
1) Objective

Demonstrate a deployable, non-weaponized “dual-channel EM gating” system that creates a controlled electromagnetic environment around a protected zone to:

Improve resilience of critical electronics against EMI/RFI disturbances (intentional or incidental)

Contain sensitive emissions (reduce leakage / susceptibility)

Detect nearby drone-related RF activity and correlate it with optical/acoustic cues

Automate defensive actions that are lawful: logging, alarms, access control changes, lighting changes, routing people to safe areas, etc.

Scope: This PoC does not attempt to interfere with, jam, or disable drones.

2) Threat Model

Threats addressed

Drone presence near a facility causing:

privacy/security risks (cameras)

RF noise / EMI impacts on Wi-Fi, comms, sensors

operational disruption from proximity

Constraints

Defensive-only: do not transmit in a way intended to disrupt third-party devices

Favor passive sensing + internal hardening + controlled EMI environment

3) Concept Summary: “Dual Channel” Means Two Independent Defensive Layers
Channel A — Passive/Shielding & Filtering Layer (Contain + Harden)

RF shielding concepts (partial Faraday enclosure, conductive mesh, shielding panels)

Cable management, grounding, ferrites, filters, surge protection

“Quiet zone” design for critical electronics (controllers, comms, sensors)

Goal: even if the RF environment becomes noisy, your systems remain stable.

Channel B — Active Field Conditioning Layer (Internal “Gating” for Your Gear)

Instead of affecting external drones, the “gating field” is used to:

create a controlled internal field in a shielded test/operational bay

rapidly switch sensitive devices between modes:

normal operation

hardened mode (reduced bandwidth, alternate frequency hopping, reduced antenna gain, fallback comms)

safe mode (temporary shutdown of nonessential radios)

Goal: treat high-EMI conditions like a fire alarm: you don’t fight the fire with a bigger fire — you move into a safer configuration.

4) PoC System Architecture
4.1 Physical Layout

Protected Zone (PZ): the area you care about (control room, lab, server rack, event space)

Shield Boundary: conductive mesh curtains/panels or a partial enclosure around PZ

Conditioning Bay (optional): a small shielded “equipment nook” where critical gear sits

4.2 Sensor Suite (Detection, not interference)

RF spectrum monitoring (receive-only): detect unusual activity in common drone/control bands

Optical camera (computer vision optional): detect drone silhouette/motion

Acoustic sensor: rotor frequency signatures (helpful at short range)

Time correlation: tie RF anomalies to optical/acoustic confirmations

4.3 Control + Response (Defensive Actions)

When the system detects elevated probability of drone presence:

log event + timestamped RF snapshots

trigger local alarms / operator notification

automatically shift your equipment to “hardened mode”

optionally activate physical mitigations:

close blinds/curtains

switch lighting patterns (visibility reduction for sensitive areas)

restrict door access (if appropriate)

route people away from windows/roof access

5) Defensive “EM Gating” — What It Means in This PoC

Your “dual-channel gating formation” becomes:

Channel A: reduce coupling between the outside RF environment and your electronics (shielding/filtering)

Channel B: internally gate your own systems’ vulnerability (mode switching + internal field conditioning in a controlled enclosure)

This is analogous to:

how data centers handle power disturbances (UPS + graceful degradation)

how RF labs prevent leakage (shielded rooms + filtering)

how safety systems respond to uncertain threats (detect → confirm → safe mode)

6) Lab Test Plan
Phase 1 — Baseline Susceptibility Mapping

Goal: measure how your electronics behave under normal RF noise.

Measure: packet loss, latency spikes, sensor error rates, resets, control-loop instability

Output: baseline “EMI tolerance envelope”

Phase 2 — Shield Boundary Effectiveness (Channel A)

Goal: quantify improvement from passive shielding/filtering.

Compare system stability metrics with and without boundary in place

Measure: attenuation of external RF into PZ (receive-only probes inside/outside)

Phase 3 — “Hardened Mode” Gating (Channel B)

Goal: demonstrate that upon detection triggers, your system:

switches modes fast and reliably

becomes measurably more stable under noisy conditions

retains essential functions

Metrics:

time-to-safe-mode

reduction in error rate under high-noise periods

recovery behavior after threat ends

Phase 4 — Multi-sensor Detection Confidence

Goal: reduce false positives.

Create a classifier score from RF+optical+acoustic correlations

Validate:

known benign RF events don’t trigger full response

multi-sensor agreement triggers logging + hardening

Mandatory Null Tests

RF anomaly without optical/acoustic confirmation → log only (no aggressive responses)

optical false positives (birds/fans) without RF signature → log only

controlled EMI in lab with shield boundary → verify improvement is due to shielding, not coincidence

7) Success Criteria (PoC Pass/Fail)

Pass if:

Shield boundary reduces measured RF ingress into PZ by a meaningful margin (defined for your use case)

Critical systems maintain stable operation significantly better in the shielded/hardened configuration vs baseline

Detection system achieves a practical false-positive rate with a clear confidence threshold

Automated defensive actions are reliable and reversible, with complete audit logging

8) Safety, Legal, and Compliance Notes

This PoC is explicitly receive-only for detection and internal for conditioning/hardening.

No features are designed to interfere with third-party radios or drones.

If you later operate in an environment requiring counter-UAS action, that should be handled only under appropriate legal authority and certified systems.

9) Deliverables

PoC report with:

attenuation measurements (inside vs outside)

stability metrics (before/after)

detection confidence curves and event logs

operational playbook (“what happens when alert triggers”)

Bill-of-material categories (shielding panels, filters, sensors, logging unit)

Deployment guidance: “storm shelter” style protected zone layout
