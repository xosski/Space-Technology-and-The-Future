POC Name: Directional Magneto-Capacitive Field Sensor
Concept

A dish-shaped passive/active sensor that uses opposing charged plates and a ferromagnetic core to detect changes in local electric and magnetic fields.

The sensor is designed to be directionally sensitive, with the open dish side acting as the preferred detection direction.

Core Hypothesis

If two conductive plates are held at opposite electrical potentials with ferromagnetic material between them, then nearby electromagnetic disturbances may alter:

capacitance
inductance
phase response
magnetic permeability
hysteresis behavior
field symmetry

A dish-shaped geometry may bias those changes toward one direction, allowing the device to behave like a directional field sensor.

Functional Layers
1. Positive Conductive Plate

Creates one side of the electric field.

Possible materials:

copper
aluminum
brass
conductive graphite coating
2. Negative Conductive Plate

Creates the opposing electrical potential.

Purpose:

completes capacitive field structure
allows capacitance measurement
supports pulsed field testing
3. Ferromagnetic Core

Placed between plates.

Possible materials:

ferrite sheet
soft iron powder composite
silicon steel shim
magnetorheological material
ferrite-loaded epoxy

Purpose:

magnetic-field response
permeability shift detection
hysteresis measurement
EM disturbance coupling
4. Dish Geometry

The plates and core are shaped as a shallow concave dish.

Purpose:

directional field bias
increased surface exposure
partial focusing of external disturbances
improved angle-dependent sensitivity
Operating Modes
Passive Mode

The device does not transmit intentionally.

It measures:

ambient EM changes
nearby magnetic fields
moving metal disturbances
RF noise
electrostatic field shifts

Best for stealthy/local sensing.

Active Low-Power Mode

The device applies controlled pulses to the plates or coil.

It measures response changes after each pulse.

Measured values:

decay time
voltage response
capacitance shift
inductance shift
phase delay
magnetic relaxation

Test Procedure
1. Record baseline with no target nearby.
2. Move metal object directly in front of dish.
3. Repeat at left/right angles.
4. Repeat with magnetic object.
5. Pulse sensor at low voltage.
6. Record response curve after each pulse.
7. Compare target data against baseline.
8. Plot signal strength vs distance and angle.
Success Conditions

The POC succeeds if it can show:

measurable field change when an object approaches
stronger response from the dish-facing direction
different signatures for ferrous vs non-ferrous metals
detectable changes between passive and pulsed modes
repeatable readings above noise floor
Expected Results

Ferrous metal should produce stronger magnetic response.

Conductive non-ferrous metal may produce capacitive or eddy-current effects.

Powered electronics may produce unstable EM signatures.

The dish should show higher sensitivity along its open axis than from the rear or side.

Practical Applications

Research Position

This is not a full radar system by itself.

It is a directional field-anomaly sensor that may complement radar, IR, optical, or magnetic detection systems.

The dish does not shout.

It listens for the shape of disturbance.
