Abstract

This white paper presents a Proof of Concept (PoC) for the Lazarus Spin Drive, a spacecraft momentum-control approach that uses magnetically actuated deployable arms to reposition thrusters farther from the vehicleâ€™s center of mass. By increasing the effective lever arm during deployment, the craft can generate larger control torques for the same thrust, enabling rapid and precise control over spin rate, angular momentum, and rotational stability. A key operational insight is initiating controlled thrust pulses at the beginning of arm extension, leveraging the initially lower moment of inertia to accelerate spin capture, then ramping control as the systemâ€™s inertia and torque authority change with arm length. The PoC defines architecture, control modes, safety constraints, and validation methodology.

1. Introduction

Spacecraft attitude control and momentum management are fundamentally constrained by torque authority, propellant efficiency, and stability requirements across mission phases (slew, station-keeping, scanning, docking). Conventional designs rely on fixed thruster placement and/or reaction wheels, which may be limited by saturation, thermal constraints, and the torque available from compact geometries.

The Lazarus Spin Drive proposes a geometric advantage: deploy thrusters outward when high torque authority or controlled spin is needed, and retract or lock into alternate configurations when compactness or precision operations dominate.

2. Problem Statement

A spacecraft benefits from a control system that can:

Achieve fast spin-up without oversized thrusters

Maintain stable spin for cruise, scanning, or stabilization

Execute controlled de-spin and transition to precision pointing

Remain robust against imbalance, deployment errors, and structural oscillations

The challenge is providing this capability while avoiding instability (nutation), resonance excitation, and asymmetric deployment effects.

6.3 Resonance and oscillation avoidance

Deployable arms introduce flexible modes. Pulsing can excite them. The controller should:

Avoid pulse repetition rates near identified structural mode frequencies

Apply pulse shaping (spread-spectrum timing / jitter within safe bounds)

Suspend or reduce pulsing if IMU detects growing oscillations
