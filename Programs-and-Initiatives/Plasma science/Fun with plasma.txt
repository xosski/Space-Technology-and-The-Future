Not by rotation alone.

A spinning plasma “ball” would not naturally stay an orb once it leaves the shaft. Plasma is charged gas; it expands fast unless something confines it. Rotation gives it angular momentum, but that usually makes confinement worse, because centrifugal force tries to fling the particles outward.

What could make it orb-like:

1. Magnetic confinement while inside the barrel
A magnetic nozzle could shape plasma into a compact packet before release.

2. A self-contained magnetic structure
If the plasma forms something like a spheromak / plasmoid, its own magnetic field can briefly hold it together.

3. External guiding fields
If the ship or weapon projects a field around the path, the plasma can stay coherent longer.

But once it exits into free space, without continued field support, it will disperse. Think “glowing magnetic smoke grenade,” not a permanent fireball.

Better version of your concept:

Lazarus Plasmoid Lance

A superconducting/magnetic shaft compresses ionized plasma, twists it with a helical field, forms a short-lived magnetized plasmoid, then launches it at high speed. The “orb” exists briefly because the plasma carries a trapped magnetic field structure—not because it is simply spinning.

The key phrase is:

You do not trap plasma with spin. You trap it with magnetic topology.

Spin can help shape the shot, but the magnetic bottle is the real cage.
