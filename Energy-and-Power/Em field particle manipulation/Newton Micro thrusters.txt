PoC A — Photon-Based Micro-Newton Thruster
Superconducting Resonator + Directed RF (or Laser) Emission
1) Objective

Demonstrate real, measurable thrust produced solely by directed electromagnetic radiation (photon momentum), using a superconducting high-Q resonator to efficiently generate/shape emission.

Rule of thumb:

1 W → ~3.3 nN

1 kW → ~3.3 µN

A superconducting resonator doesn’t “create extra thrust,” but it can:

increase stored RF energy for a given input power (high Q),

stabilize frequency/phase,

support efficient coupling to an antenna or aperture for directed emission.

3) PoC Architecture

Core stack

Cryostat / superconducting cavity

SRF-style cavity or superconducting microwave resonator (e.g., Nb)

RF source + power amplifier

frequency matched to cavity mode

Coupler + directional radiator

waveguide or horn antenna (RF version)

OR frequency conversion + optical cavity + collimated beam (laser version)

Thrust stand

torsion pendulum or ultra-sensitive balance designed for nN–µN

Two build options

RF-only PoC (simpler): cavity → directional horn emission

Laser PoC (cleaner directionality): resonator stabilizes oscillator → laser emission through collimator

Phase 3: Null tests

Replace horn with matched load (no net directed emission)
Expect thrust → ~0 (within noise), because radiation momentum is not directed.

Run with same electrical power but into a dummy heater to check thermal artifacts.

5) Instrumentation & Controls (non-negotiable)

Directional RF power measurement: directional coupler + calibrated power meter

Radiation pattern confirmation: near-field scan or far-field antenna measurement

Thrust stand monitoring: displacement sensor (optical), environmental vibration logging

Thermal monitoring: IR sensors/thermocouples on cavity and supports

7) Key Risks / False Positives

Thermal expansion of supports producing apparent force

RF coupling to nearby metal producing Lorentz forces

Cable forces / ground loops

Cryogenic boil-off micro-jets (must isolate)

8) Expected Results

Realistic thrust ranges:

10 W directed emission → ~33 nN

100 W → ~0.33 µN

1 kW → ~3.3 µN

This is “real thrust,” but tiny—best used for precision pointing, not primary propulsion.
