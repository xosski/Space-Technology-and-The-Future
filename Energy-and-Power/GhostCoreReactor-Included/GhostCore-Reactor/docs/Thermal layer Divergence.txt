GhostCore Reactor Thermal Skin
Proof-of-Concept White Paper: Rotating Ribbed Transport Sleeve with Embedded Buffer Cooling Channels
Version

POC v0.1 (concept validation / architecture definition)

Abstract

This paper proposes a thermal interface architecture intended to suppress localized cold spots, stratification-driven freezing risk, and wall hot-spot fatigue in high-temperature molten coolant transport. The concept uses a rotating ribbed metal sleeve within a circular primary pipe. The sleeve’s rib structure houses embedded buffer coolant channels, transforming the wall/interface into a distributed heat exchanger (“thermal skin”). Rotation is used to disrupt persistent circumferential temperature gradients and prevent stationary cold zones. A secondary “induction-coupled” circulation concept is optionally included to reduce pumping complexity in the buffer loop. The POC defines goals, architecture, operating modes, risk drivers, and a staged test program.

1. Problem Statement

High-temperature molten coolant systems (e.g., heavy liquid metal or salt loops) face three recurring thermal-hydraulic hazards:

Persistent cold spots at walls, fittings, and exchanger inlets that can nucleate freezing/plugging.

Thermal stratification (hot/cold layering) that forms stable cold channels and accelerates corrosion/fouling in localized regions.

Thermal shock and fatigue driven by abrupt ∆T at the wall during transient cooling events.

A conventional mitigation is to keep margins large (high minimum temperature everywhere), but this can reduce heat rejection authority and slow transient response. This POC seeks a geometry/control-based mitigation: make cold spots structurally difficult to form and persist.

2. Concept Overview
2.1 Primary Elements

Primary heated loop: the main molten coolant pipe (the “hot side”).

Rotating ribbed sleeve: a coaxial inner structure inside the pipe that rotates relative to the primary flow, producing controlled shear/mixing at the interface.

Embedded buffer coolant network: channels inside the ribbed sleeve carry a secondary fluid that absorbs heat from the primary-facing surface and exports it downstream (to a cold sink such as your “super-cooled plasma chamber” module, via staged exchange).

Manifolds + isolation segmentation: the sleeve is divided into segments (“tiles” or rings) to isolate leaks/clogs and manage flow distribution.

2.2 The Key Claim (POC Hypothesis)

A rotating ribbed sleeve with distributed buffer channels will:

reduce circumferential thermal gradients and prevent stationary cold channels;

maintain the primary-facing wall above a minimum temperature more reliably than point cooling;

shift transient thermal stress from “single-location shock” to “distributed moderated extraction.”

3. Functional Goals and Success Criteria (POC)
3.1 Thermal Goals

No stationary cold zones on the primary-facing surface during steady-state and transient cooling.

Minimum primary-side wall temperature margin maintained (design constraint, not optional).

Peak wall thermal gradient reduced vs a non-rotating / non-distributed interface.

3.2 Hydraulic / Operations Goals

Avoid channel blockage runaway (freeze/fouling).

Maintain stable control (no oscillatory overcooling).

Enable graceful degradation (partial channel failures do not induce full-loop failure).

3.3 POC Acceptance Metrics

A prototype should demonstrate:

Circumferential wall temperature uniformity improvement (e.g., reduced ΔT around circumference at a given axial location).

No plug formation in buffer channels over defined test duration.

Stable response to step changes in sink power without violating minimum wall temperature.

4. Architecture
4.1 Cross-Section Description (verbal)

From center outward:

Primary molten flow region

Rotating ribbed sleeve outer surface (primary-facing surface)

Embedded buffer channels within ribs (“studs”)

Sleeve structural backbone

Outer boundary / containment region (depends on whether sleeve is free-rotating in-flow or supported by bearings)

4.2 Rotation Implementation Options

Option A: Sleeve rotation only (primary pipe stationary; sleeve rotates inside).

Option B: Entire pipe rotates (more mechanical burden; sometimes simpler flow symmetry).

Option C: Local rotation modules (rotating section only where cold spots risk is highest: IHX inlet zones, elbows, manifold volumes).

POC recommendation: Option C for feasibility and containment.

4.3 Buffer Loop Implementation Options

Option 1: Pumped buffer loop (most controllable, most conventional).

Option 2: Induction-assisted circulation (electromagnetic forcing for conductive fluids; reduces mechanical pumps but adds EM complexity).

Option 3: Hybrid (small pump + induction for transient authority).

POC recommendation: start with Option 1 (pumped), then layer induction later.

5. Why Rotation Helps (and what it doesn’t “guarantee”)

Rotation can reduce cold channels caused by gravity-driven stratification by:

introducing shear at the wall;

promoting mixing near the interface;

preventing a stationary cold boundary from “parking” at one azimuth.

However, rotation does not magically remove freezing risk if:

the local wall heat flux to the buffer loop is high enough to pull the wall below safe minimum;

the buffer channels are too small and freeze locally;

pressure/flow distribution causes some ribs to starve and become cold sinks.

So rotation is a bias against persistence, not an absolute immunity.

7. Key Engineering Risks
7.1 Mechanical Risks

Bearings/seals at high temperature (especially if the sleeve is supported).

Vibration/imbalance and fatigue in the ribbed structure.

Differential thermal expansion between sleeve layers and channel ribs.

7.2 Thermal-Hydraulic Risks

Flow maldistribution in the buffer channels (some ribs overcool, others undercool).

Channel plugging from freezing, oxides, or particulates.

Oscillatory control if the cold sink is extremely strong or rapidly variable.

7.3 Materials and Chemistry Risks

Protective oxide stability (for lead-family coolants, oxygen control regimes matter).

Fouling at cold ribs due to precipitation/spallation during transients.

7.4 Failure Modes (POC focus list)

Sleeve channel local freeze → partial blockage → elevated pressure drop → segment isolation must trigger.

Sleeve leak → cross-contamination between buffer and primary (must be detected early).

Rotation loss → system must revert to safe mode (reduce sink power, increase bypass mixing, maintain minimum temps).

8. Control Philosophy (POC)
8.1 Primary Control Objective

Protect minimum wall temperature first; heat extraction second.

8.2 Control Actuators

Buffer loop flow rate (pump speed).

Bypass mixing valve (warm return mixed with cold return before entering sleeve).

Staged cooling (warm exchanger upstream of coldest sink).

Rotation speed (secondary influence; do not use as primary safety control).

8.3 Required Sensing (POC)

Circumferential wall temperature sensing (multiple azimuth points).

Segment inlet/outlet ∆T for buffer loop.

Segment ∆P for blockage detection.

Rotation speed and vibration monitoring.

9. POC Prototype Plan
Phase 1: Benchtop Thermal Skin Coupon

Build a small rotating ribbed sleeve section with embedded channels.

Use a non-hazardous high-temp oil or molten-salt surrogate (depending on lab capability) as primary analog.

Validate circumferential temperature uniformity improvement with rotation.

Phase 2: Segmented Ring Module

Add segmentation (2–6 segments) with independent ∆P/∆T sensing.

Induce partial blockage in one segment to validate isolation behavior.

Phase 3: Transient Sink Coupling

Couple buffer loop to a controllable cold sink emulator (variable heat exchanger).

Perform step changes in sink strength and verify minimum wall temperature never violated.

Phase 4: Induction Assist (Optional)

If buffer coolant is conductive and compatible, add EM forcing to demonstrate partial or full circulation assist.

Evaluate whether induction introduces destabilizing oscillations or hot spots.

10. What “Success” Looks Like

A successful POC demonstrates:

rotation reduces persistent circumferential cold banding under representative thermal loads;

distributed channels prevent single-location overcooling;

segmentation prevents a single freeze/clog from propagating;

transients can be absorbed by bypass + staged cooling without wall temperature violation.
Addendum A — Bi-Directional Cooling Loop and Reversible Rotation Modes

(Append to “GhostCore Reactor Thermal Skin — POC White Paper v0.1”)

A.1 Purpose

This addendum defines a bi-directional operational mode for the segmented thermal-skin system. The intent is to further suppress persistent cold bands and inlet-biased freezing/fouling by enabling:

Reversible sleeve rotation (CW / CCW) to prevent stationary circumferential thermal patterns.

Reversible buffer-channel flow direction (forward / reverse) per segment to prevent any single end of a segment from becoming a permanent “cold-inlet trap.”

This is not “flip constantly.” It is a controlled, state-based switching system that preserves the minimum wall temperature constraint as the primary safety objective.

A.2 Definitions

Segment: An isolated axial ring/tile of the ribbed sleeve with its own buffer-channel network and ports.

Forward flow: Buffer coolant enters Segment End A and exits Segment End B.

Reverse flow: Buffer coolant enters Segment End B and exits Segment End A.

Rotation direction: Sleeve rotational direction relative to the primary loop (CW or CCW).

Minimum wall temperature constraint: The primary-facing wall temperature must remain above a defined safe minimum:

A.3.2 Mixing Node Requirement (Non-Optional)

Before entering any segment (regardless of forward or reverse), buffer coolant must pass through a mixing/bypass node so that the inlet temperature is never the coldest return directly from the sink.

This ensures reversals do not “move the freezing risk from one end to the other” without mitigation.

A.4 Operating Modes
A.4.1 Mode 1 — Rotation Reversal (Primary Recommended Bi-Directional Feature)

Run sleeve rotation CW for a defined interval.

Ramp down to zero.

Ramp up CCW for a defined interval.

Objective: break long-lived azimuthal thermal structures and deposit “memory,” reducing persistent cold bands.

Constraints:

Reversal cadence must avoid torsional fatigue and resonant speed bands.

Direction changes must be smooth (controlled ramp), not instantaneous.

A.4.2 Mode 2 — Segment Flow Reversal (Conditional / Supervised)

Segments operate in forward flow by default.

A subset of segments may be reversed under controlled conditions for deposit management and inlet bias reduction.

Objective: prevent one end of a segment from acting as the permanent cold inlet region (where fouling, precipitation, or freezing initiates).

Constraints:

Flow reversal is permitted only when:

A.4.3 Mode 3 — Counter-Pattern Operation (Advanced)

Adjacent segments run different states to decorrelate thermal patterns:

Segment 2: reverse flow + CW

Segment 3: forward flow + CCW

Objective: spatially distribute any residual cold bias and prevent system-scale periodicity.

Risk: increased control complexity; recommended only after Phase 2 POC success.

A.5 Control Philosophy (Bi-Directional)
A.5.1 Primary Control Objective

Never violate minimum primary-facing wall temperature.
Bi-directional operation is subordinate to this constraint.

A.5.2 Control Inputs

Segment selection (which segments reverse flow)

Rotation direction and speed setpoints

Buffer flow rate

Bypass ratio at mixing node

Sink staging (warm stage then cold stage)

A.6 Switching Logic (State Machine)

Each segment implements a supervised state machine:

NORMAL-FWD

NORMAL-REV

RAMP-TRANSFER (valves transition through safe intermediate positions; flow maintained via bypass path)

BYPASS-MIX (segment supplied with moderated inlet temperature)

ISOLATE (fault containment)

RECOVERY/THAW (optional; controlled warm flow only)

Key rule: never allow a segment to experience a reversal while receiving the coldest return temperature.

A.7 Instrumentation Requirements (Addendum)

Minimum additional sensing to support bi-directional operation:

Segment inlet temperature (both ends A and B or inferred through switching logic)

Segment outlet temperature

Segment ∆P (high-resolution trend)

Circumferential wall temperature points (azimuthal)

Valve position feedback (to avoid “half-switched” states)

Rotation speed + vibration signature (reversal ramps)

A.8 Failure Modes Specific to Bi-Directional Operation

Valve Stuck Mid-Switch → partial blockage / unexpected flow path

Mitigation: position feedback; fail-to-bypass design; auto-isolation.

Reversal Causes Cold Inlet Shock → local freeze initiation

Mitigation: mandatory mixing node; reversal only under temperature margin.

Rotation Reversal Fatigue → mechanical wear/crack initiation

Mitigation: low reversal frequency; smooth ramps; avoid resonance bands.

Pattern Locking (system falls into a periodic thermal mode)

Mitigation: randomized or scheduled decorrelation patterns; segment staggering.

A.9 POC Test Additions for Bi-Directional Validation

Add to prototype program:

BD-Test 1: CW steady vs CCW steady comparison of circumferential ΔT.

BD-Test 2: controlled reversal ramps; vibration + thermal uniformity measurement.

BD-Test 3: segment flow reversal under stable thermal load; verify no inlet cold shock.

BD-Test 4: induced fouling/pressure-drop simulation; verify isolation and safe bypass.

A.10 Summary (Addendum)

Segmentation enables bi-directional operation in two layers:

Rotation reversal is the highest-value, lowest-risk way to prevent persistent cold channel formation.

Channel flow reversal can be used as a targeted tool for deposit management and inlet bias reduction, but requires strict mixing and supervised switching to avoid inducing freezing during transitions.

In all cases, bi-directional features are pattern-breaking tools, not substitutes for minimum-temperature margin design.
