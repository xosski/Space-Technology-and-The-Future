PTEC EMP-Shield PoC (Home Lab Scale)
What you’re trying to demonstrate (measurable)

You want a device inside a “null zone” to keep working while you hit the outside with fast electrical noise.

Pass criteria options

“Protected device does not reset” (microcontroller keeps logging)

“Inside-field pickup shows ≥X dB reduction”

“Packet loss / glitch count drops with counter-rotation enabled”

The physical layout (3-layer “PTEC shell”)

Think nested shells:

Layer 0: The “crew” (inner null zone payload)

A small microcontroller/logger (anything that records uptime + errors)

A “field probe” wire loop (a tiny coil connected to a cheap scope / audio interface / ADC) to measure induced spikes

Layer 1: Inner stabilizer cage (static Faraday + ground reference)

A conductive enclosure (metal project box, copper tape box, or metal mesh)

This is your baseline shield.

Layer 2: Rotating phase-shear cage (the “counter-rotation PTEC”)

A second conductive ring/sleeve outside the inner cage that can rotate.

The point isn’t that rotation magically blocks fields; it’s that rotation lets you switch coupling pathways quickly:

It changes contact geometry, seam alignment, and eddy-current paths

It can also modulate effective impedance if you segment it

Simple buildable version:
A “drum” of metal mesh or copper tape on a lightweight ring, mounted on a lazy-susan bearing or cheap turntable motor, spinning around the inner cage with a small air gap.

Layer 3: Outer sacrificial shunt (“shield emitter rails”)

A conductive outer skin that is intentionally the first hit surface

Connected to a transient shunt path (in fiction: “prism gate dump”; in PoC: a safe discharge route)

In the PoC, this layer is where you apply your transient stimulus.

The “counter-rotation” behavior (what it represents)

In your lore: counter-rotation = phase shear that prevents resonance capture.

In the PoC: counter-rotation = dynamic boundary condition:

seams don’t line up for long

induced currents can’t “find” a stable choke point

the shield’s leakage hotspots smear out over time

You can make the effect stronger by building the rotating shell as segmented conductive strips (like alternating copper-tape “petals”), so eddy-current loops are forced to re-route as it rotates.

How to do the “EMP hit” safely at home

Do not try to build a real EMP device. Instead, use standard transient sources that are meant for electronics testing:

Safer stimulus options

ESD-style zap from a consumer ESD tool (if you have one), applied to the outer shell only

Fast transient injection from an off-the-shelf surge/EFT/noise module (benign test gear) into the outer shell

Spark-free contact transient: switching inductive loads nearby (still keep it controlled and consistent)

The point is repeatability, not violence.

Test protocol (clean, repeatable)

You run three conditions and compare:

A) No shielding (control)

Payload on bench

Apply transient in the same location

Record: resets, error spikes, probe coil peak amplitude

B) Static shielding (inner + outer shells, no rotation)

Payload inside nested cages, rotation off

Apply same transient

Record the same metrics

C) PTEC mode (rotation on)

Same setup, but rotating shell active

Apply transient

Record metrics

What you should see (if the concept works):

Condition B is already much better than A (Faraday effect)

Condition C reduces remaining leakage further or makes it less likely to trigger resets (hotspots smeared, coupling reduced)

Optional “phase-lock” add-on (makes it feel more like your PTEC)

When the probe coil detects a spike above threshold, the payload:

pauses noncritical loads

writes to memory immediately

Lore translation: “inner null zone phase-locks” during the hit.
