One-Line Doctrine

Cold Plasma Alveolar Preclean Layer is a low-energy ion-assisted treatment stage embedded within SPAN duct architecture to pre-charge particulates, suppress selected contaminants, and improve downstream capture efficiency without replacing the primary atmospheric regeneration core.

1. Executive Summary

A filter catches what reaches it.

A preclean layer decides what reaches it first.

The Cold Plasma Alveolar Preclean Layer is proposed as a controlled, low-energy treatment stage integrated into the Shipboard Prism Alveolar Vent Network (SPAN). Its purpose is to improve atmospheric cleaning before air reaches the deeper capture, conditioning, and regeneration layers by using carefully managed ionization or cold-plasma exposure to alter the behavior of airborne contaminants.

This system is not meant to replace conventional filtration, moisture control, or central oxygen-regeneration processes. Instead, it sits upstream as a pre-treatment organ. It is strongest when used to:

charge particulates so they are easier to capture
reduce smoke and aerosol spread
suppress some microbial or bioaerosol loads
precondition contaminated air before it reaches the central life-support core

That framing is consistent with SPAN, which already treats ventilation as distributed life support rather than passive plumbing, and with Emergency Respiratory Preservation Mode (ERPM), which prioritizes contaminant interception, breathable-zone routing, and reduced burden on the central core during crisis conditions.

It also fits the realism boundary of Prism Lung, which makes clear that atmospheric survivability depends on layered integration, not a magical single mechanism.

2. Problem Statement

In closed-loop ships and sealed habitats, contamination spreads faster than centralized systems can always respond.

Raw cabin air may contain:

smoke
dust
micro-particles
aerosols
chemical residues
humidity-carrying contaminant droplets
biological particulates

Even in a strong SPAN architecture, these still impose load on downstream elements:

capture meshes
humidity layers
duct routing surfaces
and the central regeneration core

This creates a simple design question:

Can a low-energy ion/plasma stage improve the speed and effectiveness of atmospheric cleaning by conditioning contaminants before they reach the main capture and regeneration layers?

This PoC says yes, within strict safety limits.

3. Concept Thesis

The Cold Plasma Alveolar Preclean Layer proposes a low-energy ionization or cold-plasma zone embedded in SPAN duct panels.

Its role is not to fully purify breathing air by itself.

Its role is to prepare contaminated airflow for better downstream treatment by:

Charging particulates
Making smoke, dust, and aerosol particles easier to attract to capture surfaces.
Conditioning contaminant chemistry
Helping break down or destabilize selected compounds before they enter the main loop.
Improving capture efficiency
Increasing the effectiveness of downstream contaminant meshes and condensate-routing layers.
Reducing central core burden
Delivering cleaner, more stable air into the main regeneration architecture.

That is exactly the kind of layered support logic already present in SPAN and Prism Lung.

5. Core Architecture

The most coherent implementation is a staged airflow module embedded inside SPAN.

5.1 Micro-Prism Flow-Shaping Face

Incoming air first passes through SPAN’s prism interaction layer, which:

increases interaction path length
promotes mixing
slows local particulate lanes
and improves exposure to downstream treatment surfaces
5.2 Cold Plasma / Ionization Zone

A low-energy treatment region applies controlled ionization or cold-plasma exposure to the airflow.

Its functions:

charge airborne particulates
destabilize selected contaminants
assist with smoke and aerosol handling
prepare particles for capture
5.3 Capture and Deposition Layer

Immediately downstream, a capture surface or structured mesh collects treated particles more efficiently than passive filtration alone.

5.4 Moisture and Condensate Routing Layer

Humidity-bearing droplets and chemically altered condensables are separated and routed into SPAN’s moisture control path.

5.5 Thermal and Byproduct Stabilization Layer

A local regulation shell prevents the plasma treatment zone from introducing harmful thermal spikes or unstable byproduct behavior into the breathing stream.

5.6 Duct Control Bus

Sensors monitor:

ion/plasma intensity
airflow rate
contaminant load
humidity
local temperature
byproduct thresholds
and capture saturation
6. Operating Principle
Stage A — Intake and Flow Shaping

Contaminated air enters a SPAN duct section and passes through the micro-prism array, which redistributes flow and improves treatment exposure.

Stage B — Low-Energy Plasma Exposure

The air enters a cold-plasma or ionization field calibrated to remain below harmful crew-exposure thresholds while still altering particulate behavior.

Stage C — Particle Charging and Contaminant Conditioning

Suspended particles become easier to collect, and selected aerosol or volatile burdens are partially conditioned for downstream treatment.

Stage D — Capture and Routing

Charged or altered contaminants are intercepted by the capture mesh and moisture-routing layers.

Stage E — Stabilized Output

The partially cleaned and conditioned air exits the preclean module and continues into:

broader SPAN circulation
the central regeneration core
or emergency refuge loops
7. Primary Modes of Operation
7.1 Passive Assist Mode

Very low-energy operation used during normal airflow to improve particle capture without significant additional power draw.

7.2 Smoke Suppression Support Mode

Prioritizes rapid pre-treatment of smoke and combustion particulates before they overload downstream systems.

7.3 Containment Mode

Used near contaminated compartments to reduce spread of aerosols and particulates into the wider ship loop.

7.4 Core Assist Mode

Optimized to deliver cleaner inlet air to the central regeneration hardware.

7.5 ERPM Burst Mode

Short, carefully controlled emergency operation during ERPM to improve dirty-air handling when breathable-zone preservation matters most.

9. What It Does Not Replace

This layer should not be oversold.

It is not:

the main oxygen-regeneration system
the sole filtration mechanism
a substitute for humidity management
a guarantee against all chemical or biological hazards
a justification for sending uncontrolled plasma into breathing air

It is:

a pre-treatment layer
a capture-efficiency booster
a contamination-control aid
and a core-load reduction tool

That boundary keeps it believable and consistent with Prism Lung’s layered systems doctrine.

10. Safety Boundary

This is the most important section.

The system must remain cold, controlled, and downstream-safe.

Major hazards include:

ozone generation
nitrogen oxide formation
oxidant overproduction
polymer or seal degradation
lung-irritating byproducts
local overheating
accidental crew exposure to untreated discharge

So the doctrine should be:

Never breathe the treatment field directly.

Treat the air, then stabilize it, then capture residue, then route it onward.

This means the plasma stage should always be followed by:

a capture layer
a byproduct-monitoring layer
and output safety checks

12. Engineering Risks
12.1 Byproduct Risk

The preclean layer may create hazardous secondary compounds if not tightly controlled.

12.2 Pressure Drop

Too much internal complexity may reduce airflow.

12.3 Capture Saturation

Charged particles still need somewhere to go; collector overload remains a real failure mode.

12.4 Thermal Cycling

Repeated activation may fatigue plasma electrodes, lattice interfaces, or duct structures.

12.5 Control Complexity

Safe operation requires tight sensing and automated shutoff logic.

12.6 Emergency Overuse

In a crisis, operators may be tempted to overdrive the plasma layer, creating more harm than help.

13. Proof-of-Concept Development Path
Phase C1 — Benchtop Ionization Stability Test

Demonstrate a low-energy cold-plasma or ionization zone that remains within defined thermal and chemical safety bounds.

Phase C2 — Particulate Charging Test

Measure whether treated smoke and dust are captured more effectively downstream than in a passive-only control.

Phase C3 — Byproduct Monitoring Test

Quantify ozone, NOx, and other unwanted compounds under varying airflow and power conditions.

Phase C4 — SPAN Panel Integration Test

Integrate the preclean stage into a SPAN duct panel and evaluate pressure drop, capture efficiency, and thermal behavior.

Phase C5 — Core Assist Test

Feed treated and untreated air into a central Prism Lung-style processing test loop and compare inlet stability and downstream burden.

Phase C6 — ERPM Smoke Event Test

Run the system under ERPM-like reduced-power conditions during a smoke contamination scenario and measure breathable-zone preservation.
