Emergency Respiratory Preservation Mode
Addendum to the Shipboard Prism Alveolar Vent Network PoC
Low-Power Atmospheric Survival Operation for Partial or Main-System Failure

GhostCore Systems Archive Draft

Purpose

Emergency Respiratory Preservation Mode (ERPM) is a degraded-power operating state of the Shipboard Prism Alveolar Vent Network (SPAN) in which the ship’s distributed prism-lattice duct panels shift from normal atmospheric conditioning into a survival-priority configuration.

The goal is not full comfort and not full regeneration.

The goal is simple:

preserve breathable air as long as possible when main life-support capacity is degraded, intermittent, or partially offline.

This addendum follows directly from the underlying Prism Lung architecture, which already divides labor between a central conversion heart and a surrounding lattice-based regulation structure. Prism Lung makes clear that the value of the lattice is not magical gas transmutation, but load smoothing, thermal regulation, and stabilization of the atmospheric-processing environment. ERPM extends that same logic across the ship’s distributed vent network under emergency conditions.

1. Executive Premise

A ship does not lose breathable air all at once.

It loses it in layers:

filtration quality drops
thermal balance fails
humidity drifts
contaminants spread
CO₂ load rises
oxygen reserves are consumed faster than expected

A conventional duct system mostly watches this happen.

SPAN in Emergency Respiratory Preservation Mode tries to slow it down.

Rather than running the full ventilation network in normal comfort mode, ERPM converts the ship’s distributed vent panels into a survival scaffold that prioritizes:

air cleanliness
compartment isolation
moisture control
thermal smoothing
routing of the best remaining air to the most critical zones
and reduced burden on whatever central regeneration capacity remains

It does not make unlimited oxygen from nothing.
It buys time.

2. Emergency Role

ERPM is intended for scenarios such as:

reactor brownout
life-support core damage
fire or smoke event
hull breach followed by atmospheric contamination
compartment toxic release
extended evacuation delay
limp-home operation on emergency power
partial loss of central oxygen-regeneration capability

In these situations, SPAN shifts from distributed atmospheric optimization to distributed atmospheric preservation.

3. Core Operating Priorities

In ERPM, SPAN stops trying to do everything evenly across the ship and instead prioritizes five survival functions.

3.1 Contaminant Interception

Micro-prism and capture layers focus on trapping:

smoke
particulates
combustion residue
chemical aerosols
debris from damaged compartments

The goal is to prevent local contamination from becoming shipwide contamination.

3.2 Breathable-Zone Prioritization

The network routes the cleanest available air toward:

bridge / CIC
medical compartments
inhabited refuge spaces
engineering control stations
emergency shelter rooms

This turns the duct network into a triage organ.

3.3 Humidity and Condensate Control

When power falls, uncontrolled humidity becomes dangerous fast. ERPM prioritizes:

condensation capture
moisture routing
prevention of pooling in vent lines
reduction of mold/corrosion/fogging risk
reclaimed water support for whatever core systems remain
3.4 Thermal Smoothing

The lattice webbing reduces hot and cold banding in the airflow network so that:

overheated compartments do not poison adjacent zones thermally
cold-shock duct sections do not cause condensation failure
remaining life-support equipment receives more stable inlet conditions

This is consistent with the lattice role already defined in Prism Lung as a thermal and routing mantle.

3.5 Core Load Reduction

If the central regeneration core is still functioning at reduced capacity, SPAN pre-conditions air so that the remaining core power is spent more efficiently. That means:

fewer particulates reaching the core
more stable temperature
better humidity behavior
less chaotic input atmosphere

This is one of the most important emergency benefits.

4. Mode Logic

ERPM can be structured into three escalating emergency tiers.

Tier 1 — Reduced Efficiency Support

Main systems are degraded but not collapsed.

SPAN continues broad operation, but shifts toward:

more aggressive filtration
tighter contaminant sensing
thermal stabilization for the remaining central life-support core
early isolation of unhealthy compartments
Tier 2 — Survival Routing Mode

Main regeneration is weak or intermittent.

SPAN reduces nonessential circulation and focuses on:

routing best air to critical zones
slowing contamination spread
preserving humidity stability
protecting refuge compartments
reducing pressure losses in priority paths
Tier 3 — Last-Reserve Preservation

Main systems are nearly gone and emergency reserves dominate.

SPAN enters minimal-power operation and acts mainly as:

a passive/semi-active distributed filter network
a compartment isolation aid
a thermal and moisture stabilizer
a clean-air preservation path for the last habitable zones

At this tier, the mission is no longer full atmospheric management.
It is survivable delay.

5. What ERPM Extends

ERPM can extend survivability in several ways:

5.1 Oxygen Reserve Lifetime

Not by generating infinite oxygen, but by ensuring remaining breathable zones waste less of it through poor routing, contamination mixing, or unnecessary circulation losses.

5.2 Central Core Runtime

If the main regeneration unit still works at partial power, cleaner and more stable incoming air lets it keep functioning longer per unit of energy.

5.3 Compartment Viability

A damaged ship may not be able to save every space equally. ERPM helps make a few compartments remain habitable much longer.

5.4 Crew Decision Time

In emergencies, extra breathable minutes matter more than elegant engineering. SPAN gives the crew:

more time to isolate damage
more time to reach shelter zones
more time to repair
more time to evacuate
6. Relationship to Prism Lung

The Prism Lung paper already establishes the core realism boundary:

the prism concentrates energy
the lattice stabilizes and routes load
known processes like CO₂ handling, electrolysis assist, humidity management, and thermal control still matter
the architecture’s value lies in integration, not magic

ERPM obeys that same boundary.

SPAN in emergency mode is not a claim of infinite closed-loop survival with zero power.
It is a claim that distributed conditioning and regulation can delay collapse and improve how remaining life-support capacity is spent.

That is a much stronger and more believable claim.

7. Emergency Subsystems

In ERPM, specific subsystems rise in priority.

7.1 Isolation Dampers

Close or restrict airflow from contaminated compartments.

7.2 Priority Air Corridors

Maintain breathable routing to refuge spaces, medical areas, and control nodes.

7.3 Passive Capture Bias

Shift panels toward lower-power particulate interception and moisture trapping.

7.4 Thermal Stabilization Bias

Use lattice routing primarily to preserve stable air temperature in critical loops rather than shipwide comfort.

7.5 Core Protection Feed

Deliver the cleanest, driest, most thermally stable air possible to the central regeneration hardware that remains online.

8. Proof-of-Concept Emergency Validation Path
Phase E1 — Reduced-Power Duct Operation

Run SPAN panels at emergency power and measure whether useful filtration and humidity control remain possible.

Phase E2 — Compartment Contamination Isolation

Inject smoke or particulate load into one duct branch and measure whether ERPM limits spread to other zones.

Phase E3 — Core Assist Under Brownout

Operate a reduced-capacity central regeneration core and compare performance with and without SPAN pre-conditioning.

Phase E4 — Refuge Compartment Preservation Test

Simulate a damaged ship with one designated habitable refuge area and measure how long breathable conditions remain acceptable with ERPM active.

Phase E5 — Moisture and Thermal Stability Under Power Rationing

Measure whether ERPM reduces condensation failures and thermal banding during prolonged emergency operation.

9. Measurements

ERPM should be judged by:

extension of breathable-air viability time
particulate and smoke spread reduction
humidity stability under low power
thermal stability in priority compartments
reduced contaminant load reaching central life-support hardware
refuge-zone survivability duration
airflow preservation efficiency under isolation conditions

These are the numbers that matter in a real emergency.

10. Limits

ERPM has hard boundaries.

It cannot:

create infinite oxygen with no energy input
fully replace emergency oxygen stores
guarantee survival after total atmospheric collapse
save all compartments equally in every failure mode

It can:

make reserves last longer
keep cleaner spaces cleaner
reduce central system burden
slow contamination spread
and turn a failing ship from a single-breath problem into a managed survival problem
11. Conclusion

Emergency Respiratory Preservation Mode is one of the strongest arguments for SPAN.

Because in a crisis, distributed environmental architecture matters more than normal-day efficiency curves.

A ship with passive ducts loses air like a machine.
A ship with SPAN in ERPM loses air like a body trying to survive.

That is the difference.

Final Doctrine Statement

Emergency Respiratory Preservation Mode is a degraded-power operating state of the Shipboard Prism Alveolar Vent Network in which distributed micro-prism and lattice vent panels prioritize contaminant interception, breathable-zone routing, humidity control, thermal smoothing, and core-load reduction in order to extend habitable atmospheric conditions during life-support emergencies.
